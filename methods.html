Statistical Methods

How baselines, confidence intervals and excess mortality are computed.

Data

Weekly all-cause mortality counts and population estimates are drawn from Eurostat and, for Israel, the Israel Central Bureau of Statistics (CBS). Counts are converted to age-standardised mortality rates (ASMR) per 100,000 population using the 2013 European Standard Population (ESP13) with 5-year age bins and weekly interpolated populations.

A small number of country-specific adjustments apply: Israel uses the 0–19 age group only; Germany uses 10-year bins below age 40 (20–44 observations); Sweden includes deaths recorded with an unknown week of death (W99). All 2025 data are preliminary and subject to revision. Causes of deaths separate details are in the Causes of death section below.

Three Views

The explorer offers three levels of temporal aggregation, each suited to a different question.

Annual view

Weekly ASMR values are summed across the calendar year (W01–W52/W53). Years with 53 ISO weeks are scaled by 52/53 so that all annual sums are comparable on a 52-week basis.

Seasonal view

Seasons run from W26 of one year to W25 of the next (mid-summer to mid-summer), keeping winter mortality — which spans the calendar year boundary — within a single observation. 53-week seasons are similarly scaled by 52/53.

Weekly view

Individual weekly ASMR values plotted directly, with the baseline and confidence interval described below overlaid.

Baseline Construction

The baseline represents the mortality level that would have been expected in the absence of any unusual event, based on the pre-pandemic historical trend. All baselines are intentionally anchored to the pre-pandemic period so that post-2019 years can be evaluated against a stable counterfactual.

Annual and seasonal baselines

For each country and age group, annual (or seasonal) ASMR sums are computed for every year in the chosen baseline window. A simple ordinary least squares linear regression is then fit:

ASMR_sum ~ β₀ + β₁ × year

The fitted line is projected forward to produce the expected level in any given year. The user can select from several pre-defined baseline windows (e.g. 2011–19, 2012–19) to assess sensitivity to the choice of training period. A Baseline Type toggle switches between two modes:

Trend — the OLS regression line projected forward. Accounts for the long-run decline in age-standardised mortality, so post-2019 years are judged against where the trend would have been, not where it was during the baseline period. This is the primary mode and the appropriate framing for evaluating recent years.

Mean — a flat horizontal line at the average of the baseline-period annual (or seasonal, or weekly) values. Simpler and more transparent; useful as a cross-check and for audiences unfamiliar with trend adjustment. Because it ignores the largely declining trend, the mean baseline will appear too high for years well beyond the training window. .

Log-linear — fits the regression on the natural logarithm of ASMR rather than the raw value, equivalent to modelling a constant percentage decline per year rather than a constant absolute decline. The projected baseline curves gently (it is convex rather than straight) and cannot cross zero, which is physically correct. Confidence intervals are computed in log space and back-transformed via exponentiation, giving a slightly asymmetric band that is wider above the baseline than below — again, physically appropriate.

Weekly baselines — EuroMOMO-style method

A per-week (week specific) OLS regression on weekly ASMR is noisy because it treats each week in isolation and strictly ignores the strong seasonal structure of mortality.As baselines are projected far beyond the training period,they tend to get erratic and swell beyond plausibility. Instead, the weekly baseline (kind of) follows the approach used by EuroMOMO, mitigating the problem.

Step 1 — Seasonal shape

For each week-of-year (W01–W52), the mean ASMR across 2015–2019 is computed. These five years form a stable, pre-pandemic reference period. Together the 52 weekly means constitute the seasonal shape — a profile of how mortality typically distributes across the year.

Step 2 — Annual level from shoulder weeks

For each calendar year, the total annual mortality level is estimated from shoulder weeks 15–26 and 35–46 only. These mid-spring and early-autumn periods are epidemiologically stable: the winter flu peak has cleared, the summer trough has not yet begun. Using only shoulder weeks insulates the annual level estimate from years with unusually severe flu seasons or summer isolated heat events, such as they are.

annual_level(year) = Σ ASMR(shoulder weeks) × shape_total / shape_shoulder

Step 3 — Trend regression on annual levels

OLS is fit on the shoulder-derived annual levels across the baseline window, giving a smooth trend line that captures the long-run decline in age-standardised mortality.

Step 4 — Weekly prediction

The predicted weekly baseline for any year and week is the projected annual level scaled by the week's share of the seasonal shape:

baseline(year, week) = reg.predict(year) × shape[week] / shape_sum

This means the shape of the expected seasonal curve is always drawn from the stable 2015–19 reference period, while its level follows the long-run trend projection.

Confidence Intervals

Annual and seasonal CI

The 95% confidence interval is a standard regression prediction interval:

CI = baseline ± t(n−2) · SE · √(1 + 1/n + (year − year̄)² / Sxx)
SE = residual standard error of the baseline regression n = number of training years Sxx = sum of squared deviations of training years from their mean

The leverage term (year − year̄)²/Sxx grows as a year moves further from the centre of the training window. This correctly reflects that extrapolating a regression line further into the future carries genuinely greater uncertainty — the confidence band widens with each post-baseline year. With 8–9 training years, t(n−2) ≈ 2.365, somewhat wider than the large-sample 1.96, appropriately penalising the small sample.

Weekly CI

The weekly CI uses the same prediction interval formula and leverage structure. Rather than a single regression SE, however, each week-of-year carries its own per-week (week specific) residual SE, computed from the residuals (observed ASMR − predicted baseline) for that week across all training years. This naturally produces wide bands for volatile winter weeks and narrow bands for stable shoulder weeks — reflecting the true epidemiological uncertainty of each part of the year.

CI(year, week) = baseline ± t(n−3) · SE_week · √(1 + 1/n + (year − year̄)² / Sxx)
SE_week = per-week residual SE from EuroMOMO model fit t(n−3) rather than t(n−2): one additional df consumed by estimating the 2015–19 seasonal shape before computing residuals

The per-week SE values are smoothed with a short circular moving average (5-week window for age 15–44, 3-week for all others) to remove estimation noise from the visual appearance of the CI band, without affecting significance conclusions (much). Circular wrapping at W52→W01 prevents a discontinuity at year boundaries.

The weekly CI uses a fixed-width interval — the leverage term (x − x̄)²/Sxx is omitted. The justification is that the per-week SE is estimated cross-sectionally (each week-of-year independently across training years), not as a time-series forecast. Extrapolation uncertainty is already embedded in the residuals; applying a leverage penalty on top would double-count it. The formula simplifies to:

CI(year, week) = baseline ± t(n−3) · SE_week · √(1 + 1/n)

Because mortality rates must be non-negative, the bounds are computed via the delta method (log-space back-transform) rather than simple subtraction. Expressing the half-width as a fraction of the predicted value r = hw / predicted, the bounds become:

lo = predicted × exp(−r) hi = predicted × exp(+r)

This guarantees lo > 0 always and produces a slightly asymmetric interval (the upper band stretches further than the lower band compresses). For typical values of r the result is nearly symmetric; the asymmetry only becomes visible for high-variance weeks in small age groups or short baseline windows.

Important. A year or week falling outside the 95% CI indicates that the observed mortality may be statistically unusual relative to the historical trend — not that a specific cause has been identified. These intervals are intended for exploratory visualisation and should not be treated as formal epidemiological excess mortality estimates.

Causes of Death Explorer

The Causes of Death Explorer displays annual age-standardised mortality rates broken down by ICD-10 cause chapter for 28 countries from 2013 onwards. Unlike the weekly all-cause data, these are annual ASMR values were caluculated using annual figuers with mid-year/ average populations. Data sourced from Eurostat adn form additional national cause-of-death registries (see Sources below). All countries' ASMRs were calculated with 5 year age bins (90+) apart from New Zealand, with 85+ opene ended bin.

Users may select any combination of countries, any age group, and one or more causes of death simultaneously. When multiple causes are selected, their rates are summed for each country and year to produce a single composite series.

Baseline and confidence interval

The causes page applies the same annual OLS trend baseline described above — a linear regression of the annual ASMR sum against year, fit over the chosen training window (2013–19, 2014–19, or 2015–19), with the same full prediction interval formula including the leverage penalty:

CI = baseline ± t(n−2) · SE · √(1 + 1/n + (year − year̄)² / Sxx)

Here the regression input is already an annual rate, so no weekly summation or 52-week normalisation is required. The projected baseline and widening CI band behave identically to the Annual view on the main explorer.

Baseline suppression

The baseline and CI are suppressed for a given country when the summed ASMR across selected causes is zero or missing for any year within the training window. For example, selecting only (U071) Covid-19 identified produces a zero sum for every pre-2020 training year, so no trend can be meaningfully fitted. Adding a second cause whose values are non-zero throughout the training period — such as (V01–Y89) External — raises the summed total above zero for all training years, restoring the baseline. This ensures the trend is never fit on a series that is structurally zero in the training window, which would produce a spurious projection.

Note. Cause-of-death coding practices and completeness vary across countries and years. Cross-country comparisons should be interpreted with caution, particularly for causes that are sensitive to coding conventions (e.g. dementia, ill-defined causes, Covid-19 identification vs. attribution).
